1) “This will be chaos—thousands of members can’t govern.”

Rebuttal: The amendment separates representation from physical co-presence. State chambers handle scale. The D.C. chamber remains for national proceedings. Congress already operates largely through committees; this modernizes assembly without impairing lawmaking.

2) “It’s too expensive.”

Rebuttal: Representation costs money; underrepresentation costs legitimacy. Distributed chambers reduce D.C.-only overhead (staff relocation pressure, constant travel, centralized office expansion). Costs can be capped in statute (shared facilities, standardized IT, phased rollout).

3) “Remote voting will be hacked.”

Rebuttal: The amendment requires secure authentication, public vote recording, independent verification, and continuity planning. The design makes equal voting power location-independent, so no faction can disenfranchise members by forcing physical presence.

4) “This lets states run federal lawmaking.”

Rebuttal: States provide venue, not authority. The House remains a federal body; Members remain federal officers; federal lawmaking remains federal. The amendment explicitly gives proceedings equal force regardless of location.

5) “Big states will dominate.”

Rebuttal: Big states already have the people; the House is the chamber where population is supposed to matter. The Senate remains untouched. This reform fixes House math while preserving federalism.

6) “Courts shouldn’t be involved.”

Rebuttal: Courts don’t need to manage logistics. They only enforce clear rules: invalid districts, void unequal voting rules. That’s standard judicial work (invalidation), not supervision.

7) “It will worsen gerrymandering by multiplying districts.”

Rebuttal: More districts can reduce the impact of any one map by shrinking stakes per seat and increasing granularity. Anti-gerrymandering rules remain available by statute or separate amendment; this reform is representation-first.

8) “It breaks tradition and national unity.”

Rebuttal: It preserves the D.C. chamber and national ceremonies while restoring the core democratic promise: a representative close enough to know their constituents. Unity comes from legitimacy, not seating capacity.

9) “It creates a two-tier House: the ‘real’ members in D.C. and everyone else.”

Rebuttal: The amendment makes that illegal: any practice giving different power by location is void. All votes carry identical weight.

10) “Congress will ignore it anyway.”

Rebuttal: That’s why the text uses invalidity: districts over the cap are unconstitutional; elections from them have no legal effect; the article is self-executing and enforceable in court. It removes the main evasion path: delay.
